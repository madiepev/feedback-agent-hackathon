Mars rover Perseverance dug into 3.5-billion-year-old mud in Jezero Crater and found something tantalizing: rocks where iron minerals and organic carbon sit side by side, marked by redox chemistry - the electron-shuffling that microbes on Earth basically eat for breakfast.

Translation: this ancient lakebed had a chemical energy source life could have used, plus minerals good at locking biosignatures away for eons.

What it isn't: proof of life. It's a very good place to have looked.

So Perseverance bagged samples for an eventual trip home, where we'll settle it properly. The patient bet - bring the rocks back, then argue.
